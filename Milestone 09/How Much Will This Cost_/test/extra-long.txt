Deep learning models require careful tuning and sufficient data to generalize well. Training involves iterative updates via gradient descent, and modern models often use millions of parameters. Optimization techniques like Adam or RMSprop adapt learning rates per parameter, helping training converge more quickly on non-convex loss surfaces. Regularization techniques such as dropout, L2 weight decay, and data augmentation mitigate overfitting by reducing the model's reliance on spurious patterns in the training data. Additionally, batch normalization helps stabilize and accelerate training by normalizing layer inputs, reducing internal covariate shift.

Architectures vary by problem domain: convolutional layers extract translation-invariant features for images, while transformers leverage self-attention to model long-range dependencies in sequences such as text. Pretraining on large corpora followed by supervised fine-tuning has become a dominant strategy for leveraging massive datasets and transfer learning. Practical deployment involves considerations like model quantization to reduce memory and inference latency, sharding or model parallelism for very large models, and efficient batching strategies to maximize throughput. Collectively, these elements form the engineering backbone of modern AI systems, balancing accuracy, latency, and cost.
